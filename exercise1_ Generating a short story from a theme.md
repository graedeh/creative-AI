## Objective: Practice creative writing through prompts.

1. Choose a theme (e.g., "friendship," "space exploration," "overcoming fear").
***prompt***

"friendship"

2. Write a simple generative prompt: "Write a short story about [theme] in under 300 words."

 **ANSWER**
 grace met peace on the day the rain wouldn’t stop.

She was new to town, standing under the narrow awning of the bus stop, watching water turn the street into a silver blur. Her sketchbook was tucked under her jacket, protected like something fragile and secret. When the wind flipped her umbrella inside out, she muttered in frustration.

“That happens to me every time,” someone said.

A boy about her age stood beside her, holding an umbrella patched with bright yellow tape. He angled it slightly so it covered them both. “You can share mine,” he offered. “It’s stubborn, but loyal.”

grace laughed despite herself. “Like a dog?”

“Exactly. I’m peace.”

By the time the bus finally came, they had compared favorite comic books, worst cafeteria meals, and secret talents. (Eli could solve a Rubik’s Cube in under a minute. grace could draw almost anything from memory.)

The next day, peace saved her a seat at lunch. The day after that, grace showed him the lighthouse sketch she’d been too shy to share. He didn’t just glance at it—he studied it like it mattered.

“You see things other people miss,” he said.

When grace’s first week felt too big and too lonely, peace walked her home in the drizzle, holding the patched umbrella steady against the wind. He talked about nothing and everything, filling the silence with warmth.

Months later, when the rain came again, grace didn’t dread it. She stepped under the familiar umbrella without asking.

Friendship, she realized, wasn’t about grand gestures. It was about small kindnesses repeated—like sharing shelter in a storm, and choosing, every day, to stand a little closer so no one gets soaked.


3. Test with at least two different themes and compare the style of outputs.

**Theme 1: Courage**

On the morning of the race, Jonah tied his shoes three times.

The knot wasn’t the problem. The problem was the tight feeling in his chest, the whisper that said, You don’t belong here.

The track buzzed with noise—parents cheering, runners stretching, the sharp scent of cut grass. Jonah had practiced for weeks, waking before sunrise, pushing through sore legs and self-doubt. But now, standing at the starting line, all he could see were taller runners with confident smiles.

“First time?” a girl beside him asked.

He nodded.

“Just run your race,” she said. “Not theirs.”

The whistle blew.

Jonah didn’t start fast. He let the others surge ahead, their footsteps pounding like thunder. For a moment, the whisper grew louder. You’re already behind.

Then he remembered the quiet mornings, the empty streets, the steady rhythm of his breathing. He focused on that rhythm now. One step. Then another.

Halfway through, his legs burned. A runner stumbled ahead of him. Jonah hesitated—then slowed to help him up. They ran side by side for a stretch before the other boy waved him on.

When Jonah crossed the finish line, he wasn’t first. Not even close.

But the tight feeling in his chest was gone.

In its place was something steadier, stronger. He had shown up. He had run. He had chosen not to quit.

And somehow, that felt like winning.

**Theme 2: Loss**

After the garage sale, the house felt hollow.

Lena wandered into the living room where sunlight pooled on the bare carpet. The piano was gone. The bookshelves stood empty. Even the walls looked surprised.

Her grandmother’s chair remained in the corner—no one had wanted it. The cushion still held the faint outline of her presence.

Lena sat down carefully.

For weeks, people had spoken in soft voices and brought casseroles wrapped in foil. They said words like peace and better place. Lena nodded each time, but the words slid past her.

What she missed was the sound of humming from the kitchen. The way her grandmother tapped the table twice before telling a story. The smell of cinnamon that never quite left the air.

Lena reached into her pocket and unfolded a small recipe card she had found tucked inside a cookbook: Apple Bread — Don’t forget the extra pinch of sugar.

At the bottom, in looping handwriting: For Lena.

The house still felt hollow. But as Lena traced the ink with her thumb, she realized something hadn’t been sold or packed away.

The stories. The recipes. The small, ordinary rituals.

She stood up, carrying the card toward the kitchen.

Grief, she thought, wasn’t just about what was gone. It was also about what remained—quiet, waiting to be remembered.

## Style Comparison

**Tone**

1. Courage is uplifting and forward-moving, focused on action and internal growth.

2. Loss is reflective and subdued, centered on memory and quiet emotion.

**Pacing**

1. Courage uses momentum (a race structure) to drive the narrative.

2. Loss moves slowly, emphasizing stillness and atmosphere.

**Imagery**

1. Courage relies on physical sensations (burning legs, pounding footsteps).

2. Loss uses sensory nostalgia (sunlight on carpet, cinnamon scent, handwritten recipe).

**Ending Impact**

1. Courage ends with triumph and empowerment.

2. Loss ends with acceptance and gentle continuity.
